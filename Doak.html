Main Narrative

History of the Doak Family Name

Saint Cadoc (Cadog), a 6th-century Celtic saint. The Scottish surname Doak originates from devotion to St. Cadoc – the Gaelic name Mac Gille Doig means "son of the servant of St. Cadog," and early forms of the name included Dog, Doig, Dock, and Doak. Saint Cadoc was a Welsh-born abbot who established churches across Celtic lands, including a monastery in 6th-century Scotland at Kilmadock (a place named after him, near Doune in present-day Stirling). His followers in Scotland were called "Gille Dog" (Gaelic for "servant of Cadog"), which became a hereditary surname as his devotees' descendants took Gille Dog (and its variants) as a family name. In this way, Doak and its variants ultimately grew out of the cult of Saint Cadoc in early medieval Scotland.

Origins & Meaning

Gaelic Origins and Meaning

The surname Doak is an anglicized form of the Old Scottish Gaelic Mac Gille Doig, a compound of mac ("son of") + gille ("servant") + Doig (a short form of Cadog). In other words, it literally signifies "son of St. Cadoc's servant." The personal name Cadog was originally Welsh (Cadoc is known in Welsh as Cattwg) and unrelated to the English word "dog" (the animal). This saint's name entered Scottish tradition due to Cadoc's missionary work in Scotland. Kilmadock parish in Perthshire – whose name means "church of Cadoc" – is one locale where the saint's influence was strong. It is in such areas that families first adopted the surname. By the late 14th century, records show the name in use: for example, Alexander Doge, vicar of Dunnichen, is recorded in 1372. This early spelling "Dog(e)" reflects the original Gaelic pronunciation. Over time, the name's spelling evolved: by the 15th–16th centuries it appears as Dog, Dogge or Doig, and eventually as Doak (among other forms) in later records. All of these are essentially the same name, modified by Scots dialects and English phonetic spelling.

Researchers typically trace modern lines to one of four hubs: Perthshire/Menteith (Kilmadock–Inchmahome), Angus/Brechin, Ayrshire (Ayr–Prestwick), and Ulster (Antrim/Down), the latter often being Scots planters or later migrants.

Medieval Mentions

Early Records and Notable Bearers in History

Historical documents trace the Doak/Doig family in various roles. After the 1372 mention of Alexander Doge, another early bearer was John Dog, listed as an armiger (armour-bearer or minor noble) in 1468. A Dene Thomas Dog was associated with Inchmahome Priory in 1478 – notably Inchmahome is a monastery in the same district (Menteith) where St. Cadoc's Scottish church sites existed. By the late 1400s, a James Dog served as Keeper of the King's Wardrobe (in charge of royal garments and treasures) for King James IV of Scotland between 1488–1498, indicating the family had risen to positions in the royal court. An Alexander Dog was a canon of Inchmahome in 1491, and by the early 1500s a John Doig had become Provost (mayor) of the burgh of Brechin in Angus – one of the first instances of the modernized spelling "Doig."

Through the 16th century, the surname continued to appear in noteworthy contexts. In 1523, James Dogge (also recorded as "Jamie Dogg") acted as a royal messenger for the Scottish crown. In 1532, James Dog of Dunrobin in Menteith received the lands of Severie in the parish of Kilmadock, and was later noted for raising men in Menteith to serve in France – likely as part of the Auld Alliance, Scotland's military alliance with France. These references show the Doak/Doig name embedded in the fabric of Scottish medieval life, from church affairs to royal service and international ventures. By the 1600s the spelling "Doig" had become common, and new variants emerged in Ayrshire: William Dowok appears in Prestwick parish records in 1623, Robert Dook served as treasurer of the burgh of Ayr in 1646, and James Dock was recorded as a burgess of Ayr in 1654. By the late 18th century, a Robert Doak is mentioned in Prestwick, Ayrshire, records of 1782, solidifying "Doak" as a recognized variant. Throughout these centuries, branches of the family established themselves in various parts of Scotland, especially in Perthshire, Angus, Ayrshire, and the Stirling/Loch Lomond area, where St. Cadoc's legacy was strongest.

Heraldry & Arms

Heraldry & Arms (Context and Usage)

In Scottish heraldry, coats of arms are granted to individuals by the Court of the Lord Lyon, not to surnames as a whole. Over time, versions of arms have been used or depicted for Doak/Doig bearers and are commonly reproduced by genealogy vendors. These designs act as historical identifiers for particular armigers and cadet branches, rather than a universal "family coat."

Clan Association

Clan Drummond Connections and Historic Events

In Scotland's clan system, the Doak/Doig families became associated with Clan Drummond, a prominent Highland clan. In fact, Doak (along with Doig, Doeg, Doag, etc.) is listed as a sept of Clan Drummond. (A sept is a family branch allied to a larger clan.) This connection likely arose because many early "Dog/Doak" families lived on Drummond lands. By tradition, the Drummonds' original estate was at Drymen on the edge of Loch Lomond in south Perthshire, and it's noted that in this region "Dog" or "Dock" was a sept of Clan Drummond, the name being taken from the clan's earliest land at Drymen. Thus, bearers of the surname Doak in that area would owe fealty to the Drummond chiefs.

Being part of Clan Drummond tied the Doaks to some significant chapters of Scottish history. The Drummonds themselves played major roles in national events – for example, tradition holds that a Drummond helped King Robert the Bruce secure victory at Bannockburn (1314) by guiding the Scottish army through a marsh. Centuries later, during the Jacobite Risings of the 18th century, Clan Drummond was staunchly Jacobite. The Drummond chiefs (the Earls/Dukes of Perth) joined the uprisings in support of the exiled Stuart kings. As part of the Drummond sept, it is likely that some Doak/Doig families were among the clan supporters who fought in these conflicts. Clan Drummond adherents took part in key battles such as Sheriffmuir (1715) and the battles of Falkirk Muir and Culloden (1746) during the second rising. At Culloden, the clan's chief James Drummond (3rd Duke of Perth) was mortally wounded. In the aftermath, the Drummond estates were forfeited for a time due to their Jacobite support. Although individual Doaks are not named in the surviving battle rolls, their identity as a Drummond sept means the family shared in the clan's fortunes and struggles in these historic events.

Name Forms

Variations and Spread of the Name

From its origin as Mac Gille Doig, this surname has appeared in many spelling variations. Common variants in historical records include Dog, Doge, Dogg, Doig, Doeg, Doag, Doak, Doake, Doock/Doack, Dowok, Dook, Dock, and Doidge, among others. All derive from the same Gaelic root and were often used interchangeably or evolved as spelling standardized. The spelling "Doig" became frequent in eastern Scotland (e.g. Angus and Fife) by the 17th century, while "Doak" and "Doack" appeared more in some regions and in later centuries. Even within one family line, the name might be recorded differently over generations due to inconsistent spelling in old parish registers.

Beyond Scotland, the name Doak also has connections in Ireland (Ulster). In some cases, especially in County Down, Doak is an Anglicized form of the Irish Gaelic name Mac Dabhóc. This Irish surname Mac Dabhóc means "son of Dabhóc," with Dabhóc being a pet form of David. (Notably, when the "D" sound in Mac Dabhóc was lenited in Gaelic, it produced a McCook in English; indeed, McCook is considered another rendering of the same Irish name.) The Mac Dabhóc family has a different origin – it has been linked to the Anglo-Norman Burke (de Burgh) family in Connacht, Ireland. However, many of the Doak families found in Ulster are believed to be Scottish transplants (Planter or immigrant families) rather than native Irish. Genealogists note that in Antrim and Down, the Doaks were more likely Scottish-origin Doig septs, since in those areas the name "usually appears as Doig" historically. In summary, some Irish Doaks may descend from Gaelic MacDabhóc roots, but a great number trace back to Scottish forebears who moved to Ulster, especially during the 1600s settlement of Ulster.

It is also worth mentioning that one source notes a rare English origin of the surname: in England, Doak has occasionally appeared as a variant of the surname Duke. This seems to have been an uncommon and likely coincidental usage (possibly a mis-spelling in certain records). The dominant narrative of the name's history points back to the Scottish Gaelic and Celtic saint origin.

Global Movement

Emigration & Diaspora

From the 17th century onward, Doak lines moved from Scotland into Ulster (notably Antrim and Down) as part of the Scots‑Irish current, and later across the Atlantic and to the southern hemisphere. Records note a Robert Doak arriving in New England in 1719. Through the 18th–19th centuries, additional arrivals appear in shipping lists for New York and Canada, and later for Australia and New Zealand. These migrations carried the Perthshire/Angus/Ulster strands of the surname into new communities where the name remains established today.

Typical research path: parish registers and tax rolls in Perthshire/Angus → plantation/estate papers in Ulster → passenger lists and land grants in North America or Australasia.

Today, the surname Doak is most concentrated in the United States (over 4,000 bearers), with notable clusters in Texas, Ohio, and Missouri, and the highest per-capita density in Montana. Significant populations also exist in Canada, Australia, and Northern Ireland. Genetic data from 23andMe confirms the European ancestry of Doak bearers, with the dominant paternal haplogroup being R-P311 — a lineage widespread among populations of northwestern European descent. In Ireland, 54% of Doak families identify as Presbyterian, consistent with their Scots-Irish planter origins.

Distinguished Names

Notable Bearers of the Doak Name

From frontier educators to cabinet ministers, from baseball innovators to scholars who dined with Robert Burns — bearers of the Doak and Doig name have left distinctive marks well beyond Scotland's borders. The figures below represent some of the most historically significant.

1749 – 1830

Rev. Samuel Doak

Presbyterian Minister, Educator & Pioneer

Born in Augusta County, Virginia, to Scotch-Irish immigrants, Samuel Doak became one of the most consequential figures in early American frontier history. A Princeton graduate, he crossed the Appalachians into what is now Tennessee and in 1780 founded Salem Church and a school that would be chartered as Martin Academy in 1783 — the first chartered school in the trans-Appalachian region.

In 1795, Martin Academy was elevated to become Washington College, with Doak as its first president. He later founded Tusculum Academy (now Tusculum University), making him the father of two enduring institutions.

His most famous moment came at Sycamore Shoals in September 1780, when he delivered a fiery sermon to the assembled Overmountain Men as they mustered for the march that would culminate in the pivotal Patriot victory at the Battle of Kings Mountain. He was also a delegate to the convention that formed the short-lived State of Franklin in 1784, and was an early voice in the movement for the abolition of slavery.

⛪ Founded two colleges & multiple churches across the frontier
1719 – 1800

Dr David Doig FRSE LLD

Scholar, Philologist & Educator — Stirling

Born at Mill of Melgund in Aberlemno, Angus, David Doig won a Latin bursary to the University of St Andrews. After teaching at parish schools across Fife, he was appointed Rector of Stirling Grammar School (now Stirling High School) — a post he held for over 40 years from 1760 to 1800.

Doig was a formidable intellectual: he contributed the articles on Mythology, Mysteries, and Philology to the third edition of the Encyclopædia Britannica, was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society of Edinburgh, and published philosophical works challenging Lord Kames. He is also believed to have been the inventor of the tartan pattern now associated with Burberry.

In August 1787, the poet Robert Burns dined with Doig during his Highland tour. Burns described him as "a queerish figure and something of a pedant" — though for many years afterwards, 27 August was celebrated as Burns Night in Stirling in honour of that very dinner. The town erected a marble monument to Doig's memory with a Latin epitaph he had composed himself.

📖 Encyclopædia Britannica contributor · FRSE
1882 – 1933

William N. Doak

U.S. Secretary of Labor under President Hoover

Born in Rural Retreat, Virginia, William Nuckles Doak rose through the American labour movement as Vice-President of the Brotherhood of Railroad Trainmen (1916–1928), where he earned a reputation as a skilled mediator between management and unions.

On 9 December 1930, President Hoover appointed Doak as the third United States Secretary of Labor. His most enduring achievement in office was championing the Davis–Bacon Act of 1931, which established prevailing-wage requirements on federally funded construction — legislation that remains in force today. He also introduced a five-day work week within the Department of Labor in 1932.

Doak served until the end of Hoover's administration in March 1933 and passed away from cardiovascular disease just months later, at age 50.

🏛 3rd U.S. Secretary of Labor · Davis–Bacon Act champion
1891 – 1954

Bill "Spittin' Bill" Doak

MLB Pitcher & Inventor of the Modern Baseball Glove

William Leopold Doak pitched 16 seasons in Major League Baseball, primarily for the St. Louis Cardinals (1913–1924, 1929). He won 169 career games, led the National League in ERA twice (1914 and 1921), and threw 32 shutouts for St. Louis — second only to Bob Gibson in franchise history. As one of 17 pitchers grandfathered when the spitball was banned in 1920, he earned the nickname "Spittin' Bill."

Yet Doak's greatest legacy lies off the mound. In 1919, he approached the Rawlings Sporting Goods Company with a revolutionary idea: a laced web between the thumb and forefinger that would create a natural pocket for catching. The Bill Doak glove, first sold in 1920, replaced all prior designs and became the basis for every modern baseball glove. Rawlings produced versions of his design until 1953, and Doak earned up to $25,000 a year in royalties — often more than his playing salary.

In 2016, The Sporting News ranked him among the 40 most important people in baseball history.

⚾ 169 wins · 2× ERA champion · Revolutionised the baseball glove
Born 1967

David Doak

Video Game Designer — GoldenEye 007

Born in Northern Ireland, David Doak began his career at Rare, where he was a key designer and character artist on GoldenEye 007 (1997) for the Nintendo 64 — one of the most influential first-person shooters ever made and a landmark in multiplayer gaming. He went on to co-found Free Radical Design, the studio behind the TimeSplitters series and Haze.

🎮 GoldenEye 007 · TimeSplitters · Free Radical Design
1940 – 2019

Wade Doak

Marine Conservationist & Author — New Zealand

A pioneering New Zealand scuba diver, underwater photographer, filmmaker, and marine conservationist, Wade Doak authored numerous books on dolphins, reef ecosystems, and ocean conservation. His decades of fieldwork around the coast of New Zealand made him one of the country's foremost advocates for marine protection and interspecies communication research. His work exemplifies the global reach of the Doak diaspora in the southern hemisphere.

Legacy

Legacy and Conclusion

Throughout its history, the name Doak has been tied to Scotland's cultural and religious heritage. Its very meaning preserves the memory of Saint Cadoc and the early Christian influence in Scotland. The Doaks, as part of Clan Drummond, participated indirectly in clan wars and national struggles, from medieval court life to the Jacobite rebellions. Over time the family name spread beyond Perthshire – north into Angus and the Lowlands, west into Ulster, and eventually overseas. By the 18th and 19th centuries, bearers of the name emigrated further afield; for instance, records show a Robert Doak arriving in New England in 1719, and others later settling in Canada, Australia, and elsewhere as the Scottish diaspora expanded.

The legacy of individual Doaks and Doigs speaks to this breadth: a frontier minister who helped forge Tennessee's earliest institutions of higher learning; a scholar in Stirling whose intellect impressed the Encyclopædia Britannica and whose dinner table hosted Robert Burns; a cabinet secretary who shaped American labour law during the Great Depression; and a baseball pitcher whose sketch on a napkin revolutionised the most iconic piece of sporting equipment in America. From the misty kirkyards of Kilmadock to the battlefields of Jacobite Scotland and beyond, the Doak name's journey is interwoven with significant events and traditions of Scottish, Celtic, and global history.
